# Most People's Problem Isn't Execution But Direction

Conventional wisdom tells us: execution determines success, anyone can think strategy.

But the truth might be the exact opposite.

**Why?**

Observation 1: Kodak invented the world's first digital camera in 1975, had perfect execution (camera R&D took only 1 year), but chose to suppress the technology to protect film business. 2012 bankruptcy, $6B market cap loss. Perfect execution, fatal direction.

Observation 2: Nokia 2007-2010, still #1 in phone shipments (execution data: 40% market share, 450M units/year), but insisted on Symbian vs investing in smartphones. 2013 sold to Microsoft for $7.2B, peak market cap was $300B. Unbeatable execution, completely wrong direction.

Observation 3: Harvard Business Review 2019 study analyzed 500 bankrupt companies: 83% had "good or excellent" execution ratings, but 71% made fundamental strategic direction errors. Conclusion: execution is necessary condition, direction is sufficient condition.

**Root Cause:**

Climbing the wrong mountain faster just gets you further from the right destination.

This metaphor reveals brutal truth:
- Execution solves "speed problem": efficiency from point A to B
- Direction solves "destination problem": whether B is where you should actually go

If B itself is wrong, execution only gets you faster to the wrong endpoint.

**Core Insights:**

ğŸ’¡ Execution ROI is linear (20% better execution â†’ 20% better results), direction ROI is exponential (right direction â†’ 10x results, wrong direction â†’ zero)

ğŸ’¡ With strategic error, perfect execution is a liability. It makes organizations burn resources faster, sink deeper into sunk cost fallacy, harder to pivot

ğŸ’¡ Signals identifying "directional error": your effort produces diminishing not increasing returns; smartest people in industry are leaving; your strengths are becoming era's weaknesses

**Underlying Logic:**

Peter Drucker said: "There is nothing so useless as doing efficiently that which should not be done at all."

Directional errors have three forms:
1. Strategic error: doing right thing at wrong time (too early/late)
2. Structural error: your business model contradicts trends
3. Cognitive error: applying past success patterns to future new problems

**Case Studies:**

ğŸ“Œ Case 1: Blockbuster's "Perfect Execution, Wrong Strategy" (2000-2010)
- 2000-2004, Blockbuster execution data impressive:
  - 9,000 stores (most ever)
  - 84% customer satisfaction (industry highest)
  - 48-hour new release stocking (industry fastest)
  - $6B annual revenue (industry #1)
- But strategic direction fatally wrong:
  - 2000 had opportunity to buy Netflix for $50M, CEO thought "online rental has no future"
  - Continued investing $500M in new stores vs technology
  - 2004 launched online service, 7 years behind Netflix
- Result: 2010 bankruptcy, Netflix market cap $250B (2024)
- Lesson: Executing well on doomed business model just means dying slower

ğŸ“Œ Case 2: My Personal Failure - "Productivity Tool" Startup (2018-2020)
- Background: 2018 my team developed Chrome extension to help users "manage browser tabs more efficiently"
- Execution data (we did well):
  - 3 months to MVP
  - 6 months to 50K users
  - NPS score 72 (users very satisfied)
  - 38% DAU (industry excellent)
- But directionally fatally wrong:
  - We optimized a "wrong workflow" (tab management shouldn't exist)
  - Real trend was "AI automation" not "manual efficiency tools"
  - We spent 2 years optimizing manual process, competitors surpassed us in 3 months with AI
- Financial result: Invested $180K (2 years opportunity cost + dev cost), revenue $12K, ROI -93%
- Cognitive breakthrough: 2020 realized problem, immediately stopped optimizing execution, spent 3 months researching direction, pivoted to AI-assisted writing, achieved profitability in 2021

ğŸ“Œ Case 3: Amazon's "Strategic Patience" vs Traditional Retail's "Execution Anxiety" (1997-2015)
- Comparative data (2000):
  - Amazon: Lost $1.4B, execution chaotic (delivery delays, inventory issues)
  - Walmart: Profit $5.2B, execution perfect (supply chain industry benchmark)
- Strategic direction difference:
  - Amazon: Bezos insisted on "long-term direction," tolerated short-term execution chaos, all-in on cloud and platform
  - Walmart: Optimized existing business, perfectly executed traditional retail
- Results (2024):
  - Amazon market cap $1.8T, AWS (cloud) contributes 60% of profit
  - Walmart market cap $430B, growth mainly from passive e-commerce transition
- Insight: Right direction + chaotic execution > wrong direction + perfect execution

**Actionable Takeaways:**

1ï¸âƒ£ **Execute quarterly "Strategic Health Check"**
- Question 1: If starting today, would I still choose this direction? (If answer is "no" or "probably not," this is red alert)
- Question 2: Will my core advantages still be advantages in 3 years? (Check tech disruption, consumer behavior, policy environment)
- Question 3: What are the smartest 10% in industry doing? (If opposite of you, you might be wrong direction)
- Tool: Set calendar reminder, last Friday of each quarter afternoon, 3 hours alone answering these
- Trigger: If 2 consecutive quarters have negative answers, immediately launch "direction reset" process

2ï¸âƒ£ **Build "Counter-Indicator" monitoring system**
- Traditional indicators (execution): revenue, users, conversion â†’ these are lagging indicators
- Counter-indicators (direction):
  - Customer acquisition cost growth rate (if persistently rising, you're going against wind)
  - Core employee attrition rate (smartest people sense direction errors first)
  - Competitor tech investment direction (if they all invest in A, you in B, either you're genius or wrong)
  - Industry policy support direction (policy always flows toward future)
- Threshold: When 2+ counter-indicators worsen for 3 consecutive months, this isn't execution problem, it's direction problem

- Operation: Once yearly, spend 1 day assuming you're competitor, how would you kill your business with minimum cost?

- Case: Netflix 2007 started drilling "what if DVDs become irrelevant," laid streaming groundwork 5 years early
- Result: When directional change comes, you have backup plan, not passive response
