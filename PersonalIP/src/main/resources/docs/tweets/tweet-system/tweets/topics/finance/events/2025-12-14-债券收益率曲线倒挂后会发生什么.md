# What Happens After Yield Curve Inversion

Event background: From July 2022 to July 2023, the U.S. 10-year and 2-year Treasury yield curve remained inverted for 12 consecutive months, reaching a maximum inversion depth of -108 basis points in March 2023. This was the most severe and longest-lasting inversion since 1981.

Market reaction:
- Stocks: S&P 500 fell 18.2% in the 6 months following inversion onset, dropping from 4,800 to 3,900 points (July-December 2022)
- Bonds: 10-year Treasury yield rose from 2.8% to 4.3%, 2-year from 3.1% to 5.0% (July 2022-March 2023)
- Commodities: Gold climbed from $1,750/oz to $2,050/oz, up 17.1%, performing well as a safe-haven asset

Historical comparison data (1978-2023):

Economic performance after past 8 inversions:
- 1980 inversion: Recession after 6 months, lasting 16 months
- 1989 inversion: Recession after 15 months, lasting 8 months
- 2000 inversion: Recession after 12 months, lasting 8 months (dot-com bubble)
- 2006 inversion: Recession after 18 months, lasting 18 months (subprime crisis)
- 2019 inversion: No recession occurred (only false signal, disrupted by pandemic)
- 2022 inversion: Economy still expanding as of end-2024 (longest delay on record)

Prediction accuracy:
- Recession within 24 months of inversion: 6/8 cases (75% accuracy)
- Cases with inversion depth exceeding -50bp: 5/5 recessions (100% accuracy)
- Average lead time: 13 months
- False positive cases: Only 1 in 2019 (affected by pandemic black swan event)
