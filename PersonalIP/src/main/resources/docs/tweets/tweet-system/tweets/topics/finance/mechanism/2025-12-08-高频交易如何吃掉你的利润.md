# How High-Frequency Trading Eats Your Profits

How High-Frequency Trading (HFT) works:

1. Speed advantage: HFT firms co-locate servers directly inside exchange data centers, achieving latency as low as 0.0003 milliseconds (300 nanoseconds), while retail investors face 50-100 millisecond delays. HFT is 160,000x faster, executing trades before your order arrives.

2. Front-running: When you place an order for 10,000 Apple shares, HFT algorithms instantly detect the large order, buy ahead to push prices up, then sell to you at the inflated price. Earning $0.01 per trade seems trivial, but executing millions daily - Virtu Financial lost money on only 1 day out of 1,238 trading days in 2014, a 99.92% win rate.

3. Quote stuffing: HFT floods the market with tens of thousands of fake orders per second, instantly canceling them to create a "liquidity mirage." On May 6, 2010's "Flash Crash," the Dow plunged 998 points (9%) in 5 minutes when HFT algorithms collectively withdrew, evaporating liquidity, then rebounded within 10 minutes.

Impact on investors:

You think you're buying at market price, but HFT has already inflated it. Research shows retail investors lose an average 0.15-0.25% per trade to HFT (i.e., $15-25 on a $10,000 trade). Trading 50 times annually means hidden losses of $750-1,250, excluding explicit costs like broker commissions and bid-ask spreads.

More insidious is Payment for Order Flow (PFOF). Zero-commission brokers like Robinhood sell your orders to market makers like Citadel, who profit using HFT techniques and share a cut with the broker. Robinhood earned $360 million from PFOF in 2023, averaging $38 per user "sold." There's no such thing as a free lunch.

1. Use limit orders instead of market orders to control execution price. For buys, set limit 0.2-0.5% below current price; for sells, 0.2-0.5% above current price, then wait patiently
2. Avoid peak HFT hours (open 9:30-10:00 AM, close 3:30-4:00 PM) when HFT is most active. Trade between 10:30 AM-3:00 PM for tighter spreads
