# Tech Stock Valuation: Is P/E Ratio Obsolete

Tesla trades at 180x P/E while Ford sits at 6x. Have traditional valuation metrics become irrelevant for tech stocks? Let the data speak.

In-depth Analysis:

From a traditional P/E perspective, tech stocks look absurdly overvalued. November 2024 data shows Nvidia at 65x P/E, Microsoft at 35x, while traditional industrial stocks like General Electric trade at just 12x. But this comparison is fundamentally flawed - it's like measuring temperature with a scale designed for weight.

From a PEG ratio perspective (P/E to growth), the picture transforms completely. Nvidia's FY2024 revenue grew 126%, yielding a PEG of just 0.52 (below 1 indicates undervaluation). In contrast, General Electric's 8% revenue growth produces a PEG of 1.5. Same "high P/E," completely different fundamentals.

From Amazon's historical validation, P/E obsolescence isn't new. During the 1999 dot-com bubble, Amazon traded at 300x P/E, deemed severely overvalued. However, its P/S ratio (price-to-sales) was 14x then versus 2.8x in 2024. The stock has surged 180x over 25 years - the "overvaluation" was actually opportunity.

**Key Points:**
â€¢ P/E ratio only works for mature, stable traditional companies and systematically undervalues high-growth tech stocks
â€¢ The Rule of 40 (revenue growth % + profit margin % â‰¥ 40) is more appropriate for evaluating SaaS and cloud companies
â€¢ P/S ratio provides better insights for early-stage, high-growth tech stocks that are still unprofitable

**Investment Insights:**
1. For high-growth tech stocks, use P/S and EV/Sales (enterprise value/sales) instead of P/E. When tech stocks comprise 30-50% of your portfolio, at least 50% should have revenue growth >20%
2. Watch for bubble signals: Be cautious when P/S >15 with revenue growth <30% (Salesforce hit 18x P/S in 2021, then dropped 60% over two years)
3. Diversified allocation: 30% traditional value stocks (P/E <15) + 40% high-growth tech (PEG <1.5) + 30% index funds to balance risk
