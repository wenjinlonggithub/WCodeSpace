# Follow Your Passion Is The Worst Advice

Everyone believes: "Follow your passion" is the ultimate life advice.

But the truth is: This might be the most dangerous advice. Passion is scarce, market value is what matters.

Why?

Steve Jobs told Stanford graduates to "Follow your heart," but he didn't mention that his initial passions were Zen meditation and calligraphyâ€”not computers. His passion for Apple emerged after seeing the market opportunity.

The brutal data: 2019 US Bureau of Labor Statistics shows arts-related careers (where most people's "passions" lie) average $48K/year, while software engineers make $107K. Your passion might bankrupt you.

Cal Newport proved in "So Good They Can't Ignore You" with data: career satisfaction correlates not with "following passion" but with "skill rarity."

**Core Insights:**
â€¢ Passion is the result, not the starting point: Paul Graham says "get good at something, passion follows." You won't stay passionate about things you're bad atâ€”mastery breeds passion
â€¢ Markets don't care about your passion: Charlie Munger's wisdomâ€”"Find skills people will pay for, then get in the top 1%." Markets only pay for scarce value
â€¢ Passion evolves: Naval notes "youthful passions are usually low-information noise." What you love at 25 may seem naive at 35. But skills compound

**Underlying Logic:**

Economic paradox:
- More people "love" a field (music, arts, gaming) â†’ higher supply â†’ lower prices
- Fewer people willing to learn (coding, accounting, plumbing) â†’ lower supply â†’ higher prices

Cases:
- 1M people love music, 1K viable positions â†’ 1000:1 competition
- 100K coders, 1M jobs â†’ 1:10 supply-demand ratio

Right path: Skills â†’ Rarity â†’ Autonomy â†’ Passion

Scott Adams (Dilbert creator) strategy: "I'm not the best artist, writer, or business analyst. But I combined these three skills to reach the top 1%."

**Actionable Takeaways:**
1. Ask "what does the market need" not "what do I love." Build skills in high-demand areas; passion follows mastery
2. Use "skill stacking": reach top 25% in 2-3 areas, combine for top 1% (100x easier than top 1% in one field)
3. Apply the "10,000-hour test": if you won't invest 10,000 hours practicing, it's fantasy, not real passion
