# Success Is a Probability Game Not a Causality Game

Conventional wisdom tells us: Successful people found the "right method," failed people made "fatal mistakes."

But the truth might be the exact opposite. Most success is a product of probability, not causal inevitability.

**Why?**

Nassim Taleb reveals in "Fooled by Randomness": We live in a world dominated by randomness, but human brains always try to fabricate causal stories for random events.

Observation 1: Brutal startup success numbersâ€”90% of startups fail within 5 years (U.S. Small Business Administration data). But ask the successful 10% "why did you succeed?" They'll say "hard work, right direction, persistence." Ask the failed 90% "why did you fail?" They also say "I worked hard, had the right direction, persisted." The difference isn't methodâ€”it's luck.

Observation 2: Investment fund realityâ€”S&P's 2015 study showed 92.2% of actively managed funds underperformed index funds over 15 years. These fund managers graduated from top business schools, have decades of experience, study 12 hours daily. But their average returns equal "monkeys throwing darts at stock listings." Reason: Short-term markets are random fluctuations; long-term reveals causal patterns. They're using causal thinking (analysis, prediction) to play probability games (random, unpredictable).

Observation 3: Nobel laureate researchâ€”Daniel Kahneman (2002 Nobel Economics Prize) found: People tend to overestimate skill, underestimate luck. Experiment: Traders predict stock prices with 51% accuracy (equivalent to coin flips). But traders self-assess: "My success is 70% skill." This illusion causes overconfidence and systematic errors.

**Root Cause:**

Human brains evolved as "causal explanation machines," not "probability calculators."

Our ancestors on the savanna: See grass rustling â†’ Infer lion (causality) â†’ Run â†’ Survive. Even if 9 out of 10 times it's just wind (false positive), as long as 1 time it's a real lion, causal thinking saved your life.

But in modern complex systems (business, investing, careers), causal thinking often fails:
1. Sample size too small (you live only once, can't repeat experiments)
2. Too much noise (countless variables affect outcomes)
3. Nonlinear feedback (small differences cause huge outcome differences, chaos theory)

Result: We attribute random success to "ability," random failure to "bad luck." This is cognitive bias, not truth.

Causality game vs. Probability game comparison:
- Causality game (chess, Go): Input determines output, skill determines result, repeated experiments yield same result. Optimal strategy: Find "correct method," execute perfectly.
- Probability game (poker, startups, investing): Input affects probability distribution, luck participates in result, repeated experiments yield different results. Optimal strategy: Increase win rate (skill) + bet management (risk control) + many attempts (sample size).
