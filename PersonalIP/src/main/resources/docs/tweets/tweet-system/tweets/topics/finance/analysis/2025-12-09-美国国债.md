# US Treasuries: Still a Safe Haven?

The US Treasury market showed unusual patterns in 2024: 10-year yields surged from 3.88% to 4.73% (as of October 2024), while total debt breached $35.7 trillion with debt-to-GDP ratio hitting 123.4%, the highest since WWII.

In-depth Analysis:

From a yield curve perspective, inversion has persisted for 24 months (since July 2022). Current rates: 2-year at 4.98%, 10-year at 4.73%, 30-year at 4.89%. Historically, inversions lasting over 18 months predicted recessions with 85%+ probability (verified in 1989, 2000, 2007). But 2024 is unique: the Fed maintains 5.25-5.50% rates while core PCE inflation runs at 3.7%, well above the 2% target.

From foreign holdings data, August 2024 shows: Japan holds $1,108 billion (14.6% share, down 3.2% vs 2023), China holds $775 billion (10.2% share, down 11.8% vs 2023). This marks China's first drop below $800 billion since 2000. Total foreign investor share fell from 34% in 2014 to 29.8% in 2024, a 15-year low.

From auction data, Q1-Q3 2024 saw $23.4 trillion in issuance (including refinancing). Key metrics: 10-year average auction rate 4.42%, bid-to-cover ratio 2.38 (down from 2.67 in 2023), indirect bidders (mainly foreign central banks) at 61.2% (down 4.7 percentage points vs 2023). The May 2024 auction faced rare "failed auction" risk with only 2.12x coverage for 10-year notes.

From fiscal sustainability, the US now pays $1.1 trillion annually in interest (FY2024 data, exceeding defense spending). When interest costs consume 17.9% of federal revenue (up from 8.1% in 2020), approaching 1990s crisis levels (peaked at 19.3% before triggering fiscal reforms), the "risk-free" narrative faces challenges.

**Key Points:**
â€¢ Treasury safe-haven status is being eroded by the "high deficit + high rates" combination. With $1.1 trillion annual interest payments exceeding defense budgets, fiscal sustainability is questioned
â€¢ Foreign buyer exodus is clear. Japan reducing holdings due to domestic rate hikes (from -0.1% to 0.25%), China diversifying for geopolitical reasons, Saudi Arabia and South Korea also shifting reserves
â€¢ The 4.5-5.0% yield range may be the new normal. Even if the Fed cuts to 4.0-4.5% in 2025, long-term Treasury yields will stay elevated as markets price in long-term fiscal risks

**Data Support:**
â€¢ Interest payments as share of federal revenue jumped from 8.1% (2020) to 17.9% (2024), nearing 1990s levels when it hit 19.3% and triggered fiscal reform
â€¢ 10-year real yield (inflation-adjusted) reached 1.03% in 2024, first positive reading since 2009, indicating investors demand higher risk premium
â€¢ CBO projects debt-to-GDP will reach 116% (optimistic) to 134% (pessimistic) by 2034, even without recession

**Investment Insights:**
1. Defensive allocation: Conservative investors can still hold 20-30% Treasuries (focus on 2-5 year maturities for lower duration risk), but lower expectations of "absolute safety." Consider ladder approach: 5% T-bills (3-6 months), 15% intermediate notes (2-3 years), 10% TIPS inflation-protected bonds

2. Alternative safe-haven portfolio: Gold (up 27.8% in 2024, breaking $2,685/oz), Swiss francs, Singapore government bonds (AAA-rated, 3.2% yield), quality corporate bonds (Microsoft, Apple AA+ rated bonds yielding 5.1-5.4%)

3. Interest rate hedging: If holding 30%+ Treasury positions, allocate 5-10% to interest rate call options or inverse Treasury ETFs as hedge. The 2022-2023 lesson was painful: average Treasury investor lost 13.4%, with 20+ year Treasury ETF (TLT) down 31%
