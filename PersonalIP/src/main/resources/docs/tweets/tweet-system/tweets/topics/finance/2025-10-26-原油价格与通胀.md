# Oil Prices and Inflation: The Overlooked Connection

How much does inflation change when oil moves $10? The answer may surprise you.

**Strong Oil-CPI Correlation:**

According to Federal Reserve economists' research, the correlation coefficient between crude oil prices (WTI) and US core CPI reached 0.73 during 1990-2023, indicating high positive correlation. More specifically:

- Every 10% increase in WTI crude typically leads to 0.2-0.4 percentage point CPI increase 3-6 months later
- Energy comprises ~7.5% of US CPI weight (2023 data)
- But indirect impact is larger: transportation costs transmit to all goods

Real case comparison:
- April 2020: WTI fell to $16.55/barrel, May 2020 CPI YoY only 0.1%
- March 2022: WTI rose to $123.70/barrel, June 2022 CPI YoY hit 9.1% (41-year high)
- Price difference: 647%, inflation difference: 9 percentage points

**1970s Oil Crisis Stagflation Lessons:**

The First Oil Crisis (1973-1974) is the classic case:

October 1973, Arab oil producers imposed embargo on the West, oil prices surged:
- Early 1973: $3/barrel
- Early 1974: $12/barrel
- Increase: 300%

US economic catastrophe chain reaction:
- 1974 CPI YoY: 11.1% (highest since 1947)
- 1974 GDP growth: -0.5% (recession)
- Unemployment rate: Rose from 4.9% in 1973 to 8.5% in 1975
- S&P 500: Crashed 48% in 1973-1974

This was "stagflation": economic stagnation + high inflation simultaneously, rendering traditional monetary policy ineffective.

The Second Oil Crisis (1979-1980) was worse:

Iranian Revolution caused supply disruption:
- 1978: $14/barrel
- 1980: $39/barrel
- Increase: 178%

US inflation spiraled out of control:
- 1979 CPI: 11.3%
- 1980 CPI: 13.5%
- Fed Chairman Volcker forced to raise Federal Funds Rate to 20% (unprecedented)
- Cost: 1980-1982 double-dip recession, unemployment peaked at 10.8%
