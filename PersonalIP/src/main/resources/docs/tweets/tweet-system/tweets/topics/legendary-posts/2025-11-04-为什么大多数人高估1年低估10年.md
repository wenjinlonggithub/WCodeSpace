# Why People Overestimate 1 Year and Underestimate 10 Years

Everyone believes: 1 year is enough to change your life, but 10 years is too distant to imagine.

But the truth is: We overestimate linear short-term growth and underestimate exponential long-term growth.

Why?

Bill Gates: "We always overestimate the change that will occur in the next two years and underestimate the change that will occur in the next ten." Not motivationâ€”mathematics.

Compound magic: 1% daily improvement = 1.01^365 = 37x in a year. But first month? Only 35% gain. Most quit before month 2.

Amazon stock: $18 in 1997 to $3000+ in 2024 (166x). But if you bought during the 2001 dot-com bubble, first 5 years showed almost no growth. Many lost patience and sold. Only 10-year holders got truly wealthy.

**Core Insights:**
â€¢ Human brains are linear, world is exponential: Nassim Taleb notes "humans evolved for linear threats (lion chasing), not exponential ones (compound interest, viral spread)." We instinctively misjudge long-term trends
â€¢ S-curve trap: Every breakthrough follows an S-curveâ€”early plateau (seems no progress), steep middle (sudden explosion), late plateau. 1 year shows only the flat part; 10 years reveals the full curve
â€¢ Time is the scarcest compound asset: Charlie Munger at 85 still said "the first $1M is the hardest" because compounding needs time. Warren Buffett made 99% of his wealth after age 50

**Underlying Logic:**

Compound formula's brutal truth:
FV = PV Ã— (1 + r)^t

Starting with $100K at 15% annual return:
- Year 1: $115K (gain $15K, feels small)
- Year 5: $201K (doubled, getting excited)
- Year 10: $405K (4x, surprised)
- Year 30: $6.62M (66x, financial freedom)

Key: Most quit before year 5 thinking "only made $100K, better to start a business."

Naval's insight: "Wealth is assets that earn while you sleep." Coding, writing, investing are compound gamesâ€”hard year 1, easy year 10.

True story: Programmer DHH (Ruby on Rails creator) open-sourced the framework in 2004. First 3 years, almost no attention. 2007, sudden explosion. Now millions of developers use it. Had he quit in year 2, Basecamp (valued at $100B) wouldn't exist today.

**Actionable Takeaways:**
1. 10-year lens test: For every decision ask "What if I do this for 10 years?" If answer is "impossible," don't start
2. Keep a "compound journal": Improve 0.3% daily (100% yearly), photograph progress. No change in 1 month, unrecognizable in 1 year
3. Avoid "frequent switching" trap: People who change direction every 3 years stay forever in the S-curve plateau. Focus on one track for 10 years to enter exponential phase
